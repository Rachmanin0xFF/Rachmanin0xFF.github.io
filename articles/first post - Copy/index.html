The Chromatic Abberation Rant

Published: Mar 17, 2020
Tagged with: graphics physics color

Chromatic abb

A note about chromatic abberation: I see a lot of "lazy" chromatic abberation around in photo editing programs and shaders... by that I mean they sample three times, one for red, one for blue, and one for green. The obvious disadvantage here is that for wide abberation effects, the result isn't smooth -- it looks like your scene is being lit by three dirac-delta LEDs. Or your imaginary camera has very, very precise photosensors. Of course, you can fuzz it (I do that here, but with squished density functions), multisample, or just actually implement an additive hue-shifting sampler (this would be the best way).

BUT!

There's an issue. In most implementations, these three samples are evenly spaced. They sample red at -0.1, g at 0.0, and b at 0.1. HOWEVER, WITH RESPECT TO THE D65 WHITE POINT, the sRGB definitions of "red", "green", and "blue" aren't QUITE evenly spaced in the frequency spectrum! Here are the wavelengths: Red = 612nm (+63nm from green) Green = 549nm Blue = 464nm (-85nm from green)

You could just put blue ~1.35x further away from green than red, but you'd be assuming that chromatic abberation/wavelength curves are linear (not to mention a bunch of stuff about the camera).

Guess what, they aren't: https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Comparison_chromatic_focus_shift_plots.svg

But from a glance at the second derivative of the "simple lens" curve, it looks like actual optics would exaggerate the green-blue distance even more. So, hey, more justification.

Anyway this is all dumb but it's why I pull green and blue apart in my naive triple-sampling.
